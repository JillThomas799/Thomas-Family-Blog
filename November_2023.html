Trip to visit relatives in Essex:

Wednesday, 1st November: Day 3 - Visit to Audley End House and Gardens, Saffron Walden, Essex

Audley End House and Gardens are under the care of English Heritage and the house was one of the largest and most opulent houses in Jacobean England. The grounds were designed by Capability Brown, though not without disagreements, as the information boards point out. The parterre and organic kitchen gardens have been restored.

Saturday 11th November, 2023
Visit to Port Carlisle and Walk:

Walk: Port Carlisle, Glasson Moss and Glasson on the Solway Firth, Cumbria:

Details of the Walk:
  • Parking: There is limited free parking on the road in Port Carlisle village.
  • Distance:6.5 miles.

    • Good :
  • History: Really interesting historical features reflecting the history of Port Carlisle and also the fact that part of this walk lies on the Hadrian's Wall path.
  • Nature of walk: Very pleasant, mostly flat level walk with some lovely views.
  • Weather: Sunny, clear day.

    • Not so good :
  • Conditions underfoot in parts:Somewhat muddy on the non-boarded sections around Glasson Moss.

    • Rating: 9 (out of 10)


    A recent BBC TV programme, (for those of you who have an up to date TV licence) currently available on BBC iplayer talked about the history of Port Carlisle, a little-known village on the Solway Firth in Cumbria (which borders England and Scotland).

    Ben Robinson, the archeologist presenter, explains how in the early 1800s Port Carlisle, as a result of the industrial revolution, was hoping to be a competitor for great northern port cities such as Liverpool and in 1819, work was started on a wide and 9 feet deep canal, which could carry ships up to 120 tonnes between Carlisle and the Solway Firth.

    Prior to 1800, the location was known as Fisher's Cross and there had been very few houses. The building of the canal however, led to the rapid expansion of the village. Today, the main street is made up terraces of these prestigious houses dating from the period of the 1810s, 20s and 30s and built for people who had money. Features of late Georgian architecture can still be seen on many of the houses. One of these houses was owned by one of Port Carlisle's shipping magnates.

    The remains of the large harbour construction, are still clearly visible out into the water.This harbour construction can be seen to lead into the start of what was then the Carlisle Ship Canal. The investors for the development were the businessmen of Carlisle. By building the canal from Port Carlisle they could take goods, especially coal which was much in demand for the industrial revolution,straight into Carlisle and export them to the continent more cheaply. It cost £80,0000 (nearly 10 million pounds in today's money). The canal not only brought trade but also passengers wanting to escape the industrial northern towns and cities, leading to development in the village to cater for these visitors such as the Solway Hotel and a private bath house (now a private residence).

    The canal took 3 years to build and opened in 1823. By 1845 Port Carlisle appeared on the surface to be thriving, with the canal busy and tourists bringing in additional money but things were very soon to change with the arrival of the railways. Trains could carry more cargo and were much quicker, reducing the time of transit from Carlisle to Port Carlisle from 1 hour 45 minutes to 35 minutes. In 1853, 30 years after it was first opened and despite the initial cost of the build, the canal was drained to make way for a rail track in its bed and in 1854 the railway line opened.

    However, this railway line also did not last for long as by 1859, the Burghers of Carlisle were interested in developing a new port 8 or 9 miles down the coast at Silloth.The railway down from Carlisle to Silloth opened in 1856. The steam railway from Carlisle to Port Carlisle therefore lasted only 3 years. However, the rail track at Port Carlisle was developed into having a unique use, being the track for a horse-drawn dandy car from Port Carlisle to Drumburgh which people could travel on. Eventually though, Port Carlisle's growth faded and today the village with all the historic reminders remains.

    There is also further information about Port Carlisle and the Solway Firth to be found in this fascinating link for those interested Solway shore-walker by Ann Lingard who also has written the excellent book The Fresh and the Salt: The story of the Solway.

    Tuesday 21st November, 2023
    Trip to Newcastle on the train:

    Today was our 2nd attempt at a trip to Newcastle on the train (the 1st had to be called off due to bad weather). So it was up early and a mile to walk to the station in order to catch the train arriving in Newcastle city centre for around 10am. It is in large part a very picturesque ride along from Carlisle to Newcastle as it follows close to the path of Hadrian's wall before stopping off at, amongst other places, Hexham and Gateshead Metro centre and finally crossing the river Tyne to arrive in Newcastle Central Station.

    On leaving Newcastle Central Station and crossing the road, in the pedestrian zone of Grainger Street, are the 3 statues ('Standing Man', 'Walking Man' and 'Floating Man') by the sculptor Sean Henry, collectively known as 'Man with Potential Selves' and depicting aspects of an everyday working man from Newcastle. At the top of Grainger Street and Gray Street is Grey's Monument built in 1838. Then it was on to my favourite Newcastle bookshop, the university bookshop Blackwell's , before a much needed café stop and continuing shopping. Lunch was in John Lewis and was actually really good, before it was time for a walk down my favourite street, Grey Street, and then down Dean Street to Quayside.

    A short walk along Quayside and then it was time to wander back past Bessie Surtee's House, which is a Jacobean merchant House best known as the scene of the elopement of Bessie Surtree with John Scott, later Lord Chancellor of England.Then on to the station to catch the train home and the walk back to the village. In all we walked 7 miles in total!

    Thursday 30th November, 2023
    Walk 7.5 miles: Back of Talkin Tarn, Forest Head and Hallbankgate

    There had been a period of high pressure for several days and the skies were blue, albeit very icy and with a light dusting of snow. Undeterred despite the cold, we put on our snow boots, snow gear and set off promptly to go hiking over the back of Talkin Fell to Forest Head, saying 'hello' to about 10 deer we saw en route, and then dropping down into Hallbankgate for lunch at the Hallbankgate hub.

    The Hub developed in 2016 out of the threatened closure of the local Coop in the village, when the community came together to purchase the shop and run it themselves. It is popular with locals, cyclists and visitors to Hadrian's Wall amongst others and staffed by paid staff and volunteers.We had a beautiful lunch of homemade soup and bread before continuing in light snow to complete the remainder of the walk. There is also a good and popular pub in Hallbankgate village Belted Will Inn, or for those looking for a really luxurious stay and/or dining nearby, there is the Farlam Hall Hotel.
